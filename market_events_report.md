# Market Events Report

## Executive Summary

The statistically detected events cluster into 11 research episodes, with four standing out as the main market-level drivers: a January 2026 U.S. natural-gas freeze-off, a January precious-metals blow-off and forced deleveraging, the February-May 2026 Strait of Hormuz energy shock, and the late-May cross-asset decoupling as oil risk premia unwound while precious metals stabilized. The remaining events are still economically relevant, but they are more localized, more commodity-specific, or less cleanly confirmed by the news record.

Several of the largest single-day moves, especially natural gas around the January contract roll and some livestock events, should be interpreted cautiously because the news flow supports the direction of stress but not always the exact daily magnitude.

## Main Market Events

### 1. Late Dec. 2025-Feb. 2, 2026: U.S. Natural Gas Freeze-Off, Demand Spike, And Contract-Roll Volatility

**Detected events covered:** NaturalGas, Dec. 30, Jan. 20, Jan. 21, Jan. 26, Jan. 29, Feb. 2.

The January natural-gas events are tied to Winter Storm Fern. The American Gas Association reported that Fern produced the highest seven-day rolling average of U.S. natural-gas demand on record, starting Jan. 24 and ending Jan. 30, while production fell 13% and storage withdrawals hit an EIA-record 360 Bcf for the week ending Jan. 30 ([AGA](https://www.aga.org/research-policy/resource-library/natural-gas-market-indicators-502nd-edition/)). The same AGA report states that February 2026 futures rose from below $4/MMBtu to as high as $7.46/MMBtu before the prompt contract rolled into March and prices fell sharply under $4/MMBtu; John Kemp separately described the move as a record-fast rise in U.S. gas futures driven by extreme late-season heating demand and front-month expiry mechanics ([AGA](https://www.aga.org/research-policy/resource-library/natural-gas-market-indicators-502nd-edition/), [JKempEnergy](https://jkempenergy.com/2026/01/27/winter-storm-fern-and-the-record-rise-in-u-s-gas-futures-prices/)).

This episode supports the large positive Jan. 20-Jan. 26 gas moves and the abrupt negative Jan. 29-Feb. 2 reversal. The magnitude of the Jan. 29 drop is larger than a clean economic price move would imply for the underlying market because AGA explicitly notes that the futures spike coincided with the February-to-March prompt-month roll ([AGA](https://www.aga.org/research-policy/resource-library/natural-gas-market-indicators-502nd-edition/)). The Dec. 30 negative gas event is less clearly attributable to a single dated news event; it likely belongs to the pre-Fern period of mild weather and fading early-winter risk premia, but the available sources do not support assigning a precise news cause.

### 2. Late Dec. 2025-Feb. 12, 2026: Precious-Metals Blow-Off, Then Forced Deleveraging

**Detected events covered:** Silver, Dec. 30, Dec. 31, Jan. 5, Jan. 12, Jan. 26, Jan. 27, Jan. 28, Jan. 30, Feb. 3, Feb. 5, Feb. 12; Gold, Jan. 20, Jan. 28, Jan. 30, Feb. 3.

The January precious-metals events are a market-wide episode, not a silver-only story. Reuters reported on Jan. 26 that gold reached a record above $5,100/oz and silver reached a record $117.69/oz, supported by geopolitical and economic uncertainty, central-bank reserve diversification, renewed ETF inflows, and momentum buying into physical-market tightness ([Reuters via MarketScreener](https://www.marketscreener.com/news/gold-hits-record-above-5-100-as-geopolitics-drive-safe-haven-rush-ce7e5bdbdd8cfe21)). That confirms the broad direction of the late-December and January silver/gold rallies, though individual daily jumps around the holiday period should be treated cautiously because thin liquidity and contract mechanics can amplify continuous-futures returns.

The Jan. 30 gold and silver crash is also well supported. Reuters-attributed reporting said gold was on track for its largest daily fall since 1983 and silver fell nearly 22% after President Trump chose Kevin Warsh for Fed chair, strengthening the dollar and causing investors to take profits after a historic rally ([TS2 / Reuters text](https://ts2.tech/en/gold-price-tumbles-nearly-9-after-warsh-fed-pick-silver-hit-by-record-plunge/)). Other market reports emphasized that the silver selloff was worsened by forced liquidation and algorithmic selling after an extreme speculative run-up ([Finance Magnates](https://www.financemagnates.com/trending/why-silver-price-crashed-33-today-fed-chair-reuters-panic-and-algo-selloff/), [Business Today](https://www.businesstoday.in/personal-finance/investment/story/silver-tumbles-30-below-80-after-worst-ever-sell-off-triggered-by-liquidity-wipeout-513793-2026-01-31)). The market-level lesson is that a macro policy shock can dominate safe-haven fundamentals when positioning is crowded and leveraged.

### 3. Mar. 2-Mar. 12, 2026: Strait Of Hormuz Shock Lifts Crude And Refined Products

**Detected events covered:** Diesel, Mar. 2, Mar. 3, Mar. 5, Mar. 10, Mar. 11; Gasoline, Mar. 2; Brent, Mar. 6, Mar. 10, Mar. 12; WTI, Mar. 5, Mar. 6, Mar. 10, Mar. 12; Aluminium, Mar. 6; Gold, Mar. 3.

The early-March energy cluster maps to the escalation of the 2026 Iran conflict and disruption risk around the Strait of Hormuz. The Congressional Research Service described the conflict as a threat to a chokepoint carrying a major share of global oil and LNG flows, and Reuters-attributed market coverage reported that the war had effectively halted shipping through Hormuz, with large Middle East supply-loss estimates and high crude volatility ([CRS](https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R45281), [Reuters via MarketScreener](https://in.marketscreener.com/news/oil-steadies-as-investors-weigh-up-us-iran-threats-to-facilities-eased-sanctions-ce7e5edcdb8df42c)). CNBC reported that WTI and Brent surged into early March as Gulf Arab production and tanker movements were disrupted, with Brent briefly above $100 for the first time since 2022 ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/03/08/crude-oil-prices-today-iran-war.html)).

The cross-commodity signal is important: crude, diesel and gasoline moved together because the shock was a system-level energy-supply risk, while metals were mixed because higher energy prices raised inflation and rate concerns even as geopolitical risk supported safe-haven demand. The aluminium event on Mar. 6 is plausibly related to the broader energy and shipping-risk repricing, but no two-source confirmation was found tying that exact daily aluminium move to a specific real-world catalyst; it should be treated as a possible spillover rather than a confirmed driver.

### 4. Mar. 23-May 20, 2026: Energy Risk Premium Whipsaws On Diplomacy And Hormuz Headlines

**Detected events covered:** Brent, Mar. 23, Apr. 1, Apr. 2, Apr. 8, Apr. 17, May 6; Diesel, Mar. 23, Apr. 2, Apr. 8, Apr. 17; Gasoline, Mar. 23, Apr. 8; WTI, Mar. 23, Apr. 2, Apr. 8, Apr. 14, Apr. 17, May 20; NaturalGas, indirectly via energy vol context.

The late-March to May energy events are one extended de-risking and re-risking episode. Reuters reported that oil fell about 11% on Mar. 23 after President Trump postponed threatened strikes on Iranian power plants and cited constructive talks, even though Hormuz shipping remained disrupted and crude volatility was at its highest since April 2022 ([Reuters via MarketScreener](https://in.marketscreener.com/news/oil-steadies-as-investors-weigh-up-us-iran-threats-to-facilities-eased-sanctions-ce7e5edcdb8df42c)). On Apr. 8, multiple reports tied a sharp crude selloff to ceasefire news and hopes that Hormuz would reopen ([World Oil / Bloomberg](https://www.worldoil.com/news/2026/4/8/ceasefire-deal-to-reopen-strait-of-hormuz-eases-global-energy-crisis-fears/), [Transport Topics](https://www.ttnews.com/articles/oil-prices-sink-below-100)).

The Apr. 17 and May events continue the same theme. Reuters reported on Apr. 17 that Brent and WTI settled down about 9%-11% after Iran said commercial vessels could pass through Hormuz during the ceasefire and Trump said Iran had agreed not to close the strait again ([Reuters via LSE](https://www.lse.co.uk/news/oil-falls-on-prospects-for-talks-to-end-iran-war--6c9a1eh31gke70d.html)). Reuters also reported on May 6 that oil fell sharply as the U.S. and Iran neared an initial peace framework, although analysts warned that shipping normalization would lag any agreement by six to eight weeks ([Reuters via LSE](https://www.lse.co.uk/news/oil-prices-fall-over-2-after-trump-pauses-strait-opening-for-possible-deal-h7qzi09w4xxovdq.html)). On May 20, Reuters-attributed coverage reported another roughly 6% oil decline after Trump said negotiations were in the “final stages” and some tankers began crossing Hormuz, despite a large EIA crude-stock draw ([Reuters via LSE](https://www.lse.co.uk/news/oil-prices-dip-on-trump-comments-while-analysts-point-to-supply-crunch-ge2skis7elel8hk.html), [Rigzone / Bloomberg](https://www.rigzone.com/news/wire/oil_falls_as_iran_deal_hopes_rise-20-may-2026-183740-article/)).

The Apr. 1-Apr. 2 paired Brent/WTI reversals are directionally consistent with headline-driven volatility during the Hormuz crisis, but no two-source confirmation was found identifying a clean, exact daily driver for those two events. They are best read as part of the broader war-risk premium and liquidity whipsaw rather than as separate fundamental shocks.

### 5. May 29, 2026: Volatility Spike And Correlation Breaks As Oil And Metals Decouple

**Detected events covered:** May 29 vol spikes in Brent, Diesel, Gold, NaturalGas, Silver, WTI; correlation breaks Brent/Copper, Brent/Silver, Diesel/Copper, Diesel/Silver, Gasoline/Copper, WTI/Copper, WTI/Silver, Copper/SoybeanOil, Zinc/Sugar.

The May 29 correlation-break episode is the clearest cross-commodity market-level signal. Commodity-market coverage reported that oil fell on optimism around a U.S.-Iran peace deal and a possible reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, while gold rose as lower oil and a weaker dollar eased immediate inflation and rate fears ([Invezz](https://invezz.com/news/2026/05/29/commodity-wrap-oil-drops-gold-rallies-as-us-iran-peace-hopes-gain-ground/), [Investing.com](https://www.investing.com/analysis/dollar-and-oil-slide-on-usiran-ceasefire-extension-200681160)). FXStreet described the broader “peace dividend” as oil giving up war premium, Treasury yields falling and the dollar weakening, while precious metals advanced as rate fears eased ([FXStreet](https://www.fxstreet.com/analysis/markets-price-the-peace-dividend-as-oil-falls-and-rate-fears-ease-202605282255)).

This explains why energy-metals correlations broke rather than simply rising in a risk-on or risk-off pattern. Oil was repricing physical supply relief through Hormuz, while gold and silver were repricing lower real-yield pressure and a softer dollar; copper and agricultural-linked contracts had more mixed exposure to global-growth expectations and industrial demand ([Invezz](https://invezz.com/news/2026/05/29/commodity-wrap-oil-drops-gold-rallies-as-us-iran-peace-hopes-gain-ground/), [TradingKey](https://www.tradingkey.com/analysis/stocks/us-stocks/261936749-trump-iran-us-war-60-hormuz-dowjones-oil-dxy-wti-uranium-tradingkey)). No single source was found explaining the specific Zinc/Sugar or Copper/SoybeanOil breaks, so those should be interpreted as statistical spillovers from broader macro and energy repricing rather than confirmed paired-market causal events.

## Other Commodity Market Events

### 6. Nov. 24, 2025: Cattle Futures Shock After Tyson Capacity Announcement

**Detected events covered:** LiveCattle, Nov. 24.

The cattle event is well supported by livestock-market sources. Tyson Foods announced on Nov. 21 that it would permanently close its Lexington, Nebraska beef-processing plant and reduce the Amarillo, Texas plant to one shift; on Nov. 24, both live and feeder cattle futures traded limit lower as the market repriced packer capacity and basis risk in an already tight cattle-supply environment ([University of Nebraska Center for Agricultural Profitability](https://cap.unl.edu/news/understanding-tyson-lexington-plant-closure-what-it-means-cattle-producers/), [DTN](https://www.dtnpf.com/agriculture/web/ag/news/article/2025/11/24/another-headline-another-limit-lower-2)). The market-level insight is not simply a cattle-demand story: the episode exposed how processing concentration can convert a local plant decision into a national futures repricing when herd inventories are historically tight and producers fear reduced competition for slaughter-ready animals ([RFD-TV](https://www.rfdtv.com/tysons-packing-cuts-collide-with-tight-cattle-supplies-pushing-futures-down), [University of Nebraska Center for Agricultural Profitability](https://cap.unl.edu/news/understanding-tyson-lexington-plant-closure-what-it-means-cattle-producers/)).

### 7. Dec. 17, 2025: Coffee Declines On Easing Supply Fears

**Detected events covered:** Coffee, Dec. 17.

The coffee decline lines up with a broad easing in the coffee complex late in 2025. The International Coffee Organization reported that global coffee prices moved lower in December as easing supply concerns, stronger export flows and currency movements reshaped sentiment, with the ICO composite price down 7.8% month over month ([ICO](https://ico.org/)). Contemporary coffee-market reporting pointed to improved Brazil and Vietnam supply expectations, including an upward revision to Brazil output estimates and stronger Vietnamese exports, as pressure on Arabica and Robusta futures ([CoffeeBI](https://coffeebi.com/2025/12/17/coffee-prices-fall-luckin-eyes-blue-bottle-deal/), [Comunicaffe / ICO report summary](https://www.comunicaffe.com/ico-report-here-is-why-coffee-prices-fell-in-the-last-part-of-the-year-and-recovered-in-the-first-days-of-2026/)). At the market level, this was a reversal in scarcity pricing rather than an idiosyncratic single-country event.

### 8. Mar. 16, 2026: Soy Complex Hit By China Trade Uncertainty And Crude Reversal

**Detected events covered:** Soybeans, Mar. 16; SoybeanOil, Mar. 16.

The soy-complex decline is supported by grain-market reporting. Barchart reported that soybeans fell by 60 cents to the daily limit, while soybean oil also fell limit-down, amid uncertainty over China-related trade talks and a same-day drop in crude oil ([Barchart](https://www.barchart.com/story/news/782730/soybeans-collapse-the-limit-on-monday-with-uncertainty-on-china)). AgWeb likewise tied the old-crop soybean and soybean-oil limit-down closes to fears that a planned Trump-Xi summit would be delayed because of the Middle East war, with fund liquidation and farmer selling adding pressure ([AgWeb](https://www.agweb.com/markets/market-analysis/soybeans-end-limit-down-fear-china-summit-delay-will-china-buy-corn-inste)). At the market level, this was a linkage between geopolitics, energy prices and agricultural export expectations: soybean oil is sensitive to biofuel economics, while soybeans were sensitive to China purchase expectations.

### 9. Mar. 19-Mar. 26, 2026: Precious Metals Sold As Energy Inflation Lifted Rate Expectations

**Detected events covered:** Gold, Mar. 19, Mar. 23, Mar. 26; Silver, Mar. 19.

The mid-to-late March precious-metals declines reflect a second macro repricing after the January metals crash. Mining.com reported that gold and silver fell as the Middle East oil shock raised inflation risks and reduced the likelihood of Federal Reserve rate cuts; it also noted that the Fed held rates steady and projected fewer cuts, making non-yielding bullion less attractive ([Mining.com](https://www.mining.com/gold-and-silver-prices-plunge-as-oil-shock-fuels-inflation-risks/)). Other market coverage pointed to a stronger dollar, higher Treasury yields and aggressive unwinding of long metals positions after hot inflation data and a hawkish Fed signal ([FinancialContent](https://www.financialcontent.com/article/marketminute-2026-3-19-gold-cracks-5000-and-silver-plummets-8-as-feds-hawkish-hold-paralyzes-precious-metals), [SilverPrice.org](https://silverprice.org/gold-price-today/2026-03-19)). The core market insight is that the same oil shock that lifted energy commodities could pressure precious metals if it caused markets to price higher real yields.

### 10. May 12, 2026: Wheat Reprices On Bullish USDA Crop Estimates

**Detected events covered:** HRWWheat, May 12; Wheat, May 12.

The May 12 wheat rally is strongly confirmed. USDA’s Wheat Outlook reported a sharply lower U.S. 2026/27 wheat supply outlook, with the largest global production reduction coming from the United States because of major drought and reduced area/yields ([USDA ERS](https://ers.usda.gov/sites/default/files/_laserfiche/outlooks/114142/WHS-26e.pdf?v=95396)). DTN reported that the May WASDE and Crop Production reports were “surprisingly bullish” for wheat, with both Kansas City and Chicago July wheat closing up the 45-cent daily limit after USDA projected much lower all-wheat and hard red winter output than expected ([DTN](https://www.dtnpf.com/agriculture/web/ag/news/article/2026/05/12/bullish-wheat-estimates-may-wasde), [Pro Farmer](https://www.profarmer.com/news/report-reactions/report-reaction-wheat-futures-surge-after-usda-pegs-production-well-below-trade-expectations)). The market-level insight is a balance-sheet shock: drought-damaged U.S. winter wheat tightened expected supplies enough to move both HRW and Chicago wheat together.

### 11. May 15, 2026: Metals Down On Yields; Lean-Hog Signal Looks Inconsistent

**Detected events covered:** Silver, May 15; LeanHogs, May 15.

The silver decline is consistent with a broader rates-and-dollar shock. CNBC reported that government bonds, equities and precious metals sold off on May 15 as inflation fears mounted; the U.S. 10-year Treasury yield jumped about 9 basis points and spot silver fell roughly 6.5% ([CNBC](https://www.cnbc.com/2026/05/15/global-markets-stocks-bonds-silver-gold-trump-inflation.html)). Invezz similarly tied the precious-metals slump to firmer Treasury yields, a stronger dollar and reduced expectations for near-term Fed cuts after inflation data ([Invezz](https://invezz.com/news/2026/05/15/precious-metals-slump-on-rising-bond-yields-gold-likely-to-remain-bearish/)).

The lean-hog event is not confirmed. The detected event implies a large positive lean-hogs return on May 15, but Barchart reported that lean-hog futures posted losses across the board that Friday, with USDA’s national base hog price down and the CME Lean Hog Index lower ([Barchart via FinancialContent](https://markets.financialcontent.com/streetinsider/article/barchart-2026-5-15-hogs-falls-into-the-friday-close)). Other mid-May hog coverage describes mixed or lower trading and thin cash trade rather than a broad rally ([StockPil](https://stockpil.com/lean-hog-futures-mixed-strength-monday-midday-may-2026/), [Farms.com / Reuters](https://www.farms.com/news/lean-hog-futures-slip-as-june-contract-settles-below-1-a-pound-cme-241762.aspx)). The May 15 lean-hogs event should be flagged as potentially inconsistent with market reports, possibly due to contract mapping, stale settlement handling or a data alignment issue.

## Data And Interpretation Flags

- The natural-gas Jan. 29 and Feb. 2 moves are economically plausible in direction but likely inflated by prompt-month roll mechanics; AGA explicitly notes the February-to-March roll coincided with the sharp price collapse ([AGA](https://www.aga.org/research-policy/resource-library/natural-gas-market-indicators-502nd-edition/)).
- The Apr. 1-Apr. 2 energy reversals are consistent with Hormuz-war volatility, but no clear two-source dated catalyst was identified for those exact events.
- The May 15 lean-hogs event conflicts with market reports showing hog futures lower that day; treat it as a likely data or contract-continuation issue unless the statistician can confirm the exact contract and settlement source.
- For the May 29 correlation breaks, the market-level driver is well supported, but the most granular commodity pairs should not be overinterpreted as separate fundamental relationships without additional spread-level research.
